CHAPTER III — Beyond the Factory Gate

They all once had homes. Real homes, with dirt under their fingernails and the scent of old blankets. But what they left behind no longer existed — not in a way that could bring them back.

Marko came from a mining town that died the day the mine closed. He had a son he no longer saw. The alimony kept him chained. The generator factory was supposed to be a temporary job. But time passed. He stayed. Breathing smoke. Dreaming of an embrace he'd once had — and lost.

Jelena used to study law. She ran from a system that wanted to break her. Her mother, long gone. No family left. The factory became both her refuge and her sentence.

They found each other among broken machines and broken people. It wasn't love. It was survival. They shared silence, scraps of lunch, glances that meant “we're still human.”

CHAPTER XII — Those Who Disappear

Dragan sat in the canteen, expressionless. They said he had once been a physics professor. He spoke little, but his eyes saw through everything.

When the fire broke out, he noticed something was wrong with the smoke. Thick. Chemical. The next day, coughing. Blood. Rashes.

He didn’t panic. He made notes:

“Chemical exposure likely. Group A — monitor. If symptoms cross 25%, activate protocol.”

Management stayed silent. They always did. Ilija, the shift supervisor, shortened breaks:

“Too much sitting. From now on — 12 minutes.”

No masks. No protection. They called it “temporary.”

People made their own masks from cloth and hope. Dragan calculated the exposure dose. And waited.

Then Nenad disappeared.

His locker was emptied. His name erased from records. Like others before him.

That evening, Dragan found a note stuck in his locker:

If you know about Nenad — stay silent. Your family isn't in the factory.

He burned it immediately. But he knew — silence is a weapon. And resistance, a necessity.

And it will come.
